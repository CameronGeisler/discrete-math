Note why the assumption that \(a\) and \(b\) have no common factors works here. Say we write \(a = 2c\text{,}\) \(b = 2d\text{,}\) for some \(c, d \in \mathbb{Z}, d \neq 0\text{.}\) Then, we have,

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b} = \frac{2c}{2d} = \frac{c}{d} \end{equation*}

Then, we can continue a similar process and find that both \(c\) and \(d\) must be even. This will repeat infinitely many times, leading to the conclusion that there not exist a representation of \(\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are in lowest terms. However, all rational numbers have a (unique) representation \(\frac{a}{b}\) in lowest terms.
