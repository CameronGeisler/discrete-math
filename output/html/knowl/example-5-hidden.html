Here is another intuitive reason why \(P \rightarrow Q\) is true when \(P\) is false. First, it makes sense that \(P \rightarrow Q\) is true when \(P\) and \(Q\) are both true, and is false when \(P\) is true but \(Q\) is false. Then, we have the following partial truth table,

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} P \amp Q \amp P \rightarrow Q \\ \hline T \amp T \amp T \\ \hline T \amp F \amp F \\ \hline F \amp T \amp \\ \hline F \amp F \amp \end{array} \end{equation*}

Then, there are 4 possible ways we can fill in the remainder of the table: \(TT, TF, FT, FF\text{.}\) This gives us,

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c||c} P \amp Q \amp \\ \hline T \amp T \amp T \amp T \amp T \amp T \\ \hline T \amp F \amp F \amp F \amp F \amp F \\ \hline F \amp T \amp F \amp F \amp T \amp T \\ \hline F \amp F \amp F \amp T \amp F \amp T \end{array} \end{equation*}

Then, the first 3 columns can be recognized as \(P \land Q\text{,}\) \(P \leftrightarrow Q\text{,}\) and \(Q\text{,}\) respectively. This leaves the last case as the most intuitive to define as \(P \rightarrow Q\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c||c} P \amp Q \amp P \land Q \amp P \leftrightarrow Q \amp Q \amp P \rightarrow Q \\ \hline T \amp T \amp T \amp T \amp T \amp T \\ \hline T \amp F \amp F \amp F \amp F \amp F \\ \hline F \amp T \amp F \amp F \amp T \amp T \\ \hline F \amp F \amp F \amp T \amp F \amp T \end{array} \end{equation*}
