Section 2.3 Properties of Logic

Subsection 2.3.1 Properties of Logic

Theorem 2.3.1. DeMorgan's laws.

  • Negation of a conjunction, \(\neg (P \land Q) \equiv (\neg P) \lor (\neg Q)\)

  • Negation of a disjunction, \(\neg (P \lor Q) \equiv (\neg P) \land (\neg Q)\)

Theorem 2.3.2. Associative property.

\begin{gather*} P \land (Q \land R) \equiv (P \land Q) \land R\\ P \lor (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \lor Q) \lor R \end{gather*}

This means that parentheses are unnecessary for a sequence of only conjunctions or only disjunctions.

Theorem 2.3.3. Commutative property.

  • \(\displaystyle P \land Q \equiv Q \land P\)

  • \(\displaystyle P \lor Q \equiv Q \lor P\)

This intuitively means that order doesn't matter for disjunctions and conjunctions.

Theorem 2.3.4. Distribution properties.

  • Distribution of conjunction over disjunction, \(P \land (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \land Q) \lor (P \land R)\)

  • Distribution of disjunction over conjunction, \(P \lor (Q \land R) \equiv (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor R)\)

Subsection 2.3.2 Truth Tables for Compound Propositions

In general, there are \(2^n\) rows in a truth table for \(n\) simple statements.

Subsection 2.3.3 Quantifiers

Definition 2.3.5.

The existential quantifier, denoted by \(\exists\text{,}\) represents “there exists”, “there is a”, or “there is at least one”.

Definition 2.3.6.

The universal quantifier, \(\forall\text{,}\) represents “for all”, “for every”, or “for each”.

The statement, \(\forall x \in S, P(x)\) means that for all elements \(x\) in the set \(S\text{,}\) the statement \(P(x)\) is true. This is equivalent to saying that the conditional, “If \(x \in S\text{,}\) then \(P(x)\) is true”. In symbols, this can be summarized as,

\begin{equation*} (\forall x \in S, P(x)) \equiv (x \in S) \rightarrow P(x) \end{equation*}

Subsection 2.3.4 Logical Inference

  • Modus Ponens, \(((P \rightarrow Q) \land P) \rightarrow Q\)

  • Modus Tollens, \(((P \rightarrow Q) \land (\neg Q)) \rightarrow (\neg P)\)

  • Elimination, \(((P \lor Q) \land (\neg P)) \rightarrow Q\)

Theorem 2.3.7. Chain of implications.

  • \(\displaystyle ((P \rightarrow Q) \land (Q \rightarrow R)) \rightarrow (P \rightarrow R)\)

  • \(\displaystyle ((P \rightarrow P_1) \land (P_1 \rightarrow P_2) \land \dots \land (P_n \rightarrow Q)) \rightarrow (P \rightarrow Q)\)
